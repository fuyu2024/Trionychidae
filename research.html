Softshell Turtle Powder

Freeze-dried softshell turtle powder is derived from carefully selected softshell turtles cultivated under controlled aquaculture environments in southern Taiwan. According to internal ingredient documentation, the breeding process emphasizes clean underground spring water sources, fresh marine fish feeding practices, and antibiotic-free cultivation systems in order to preserve biological quality and nutritional stability.

The freeze-drying process is designed to minimize thermal degradation of bioactive compounds and preserve naturally occurring proteins, collagen peptides, amino acids, minerals, and glycosaminoglycan-related substances. The manufacturing approach also incorporates low-temperature processing and freeze-crystal preservation technology intended to reduce nutrient denaturation during extraction and drying procedures.

Softshell turtle-derived materials contain naturally occurring protein complexes, keratin-associated compounds, trace minerals, calcium, zinc, selenium, and unsaturated fatty acids. These nutritional components have historically been investigated for their potential role in maintaining physiological function, nutritional balance, and connective tissue integrity.

Particular attention has been directed toward the peripheral soft tissue region commonly referred to as the “skirt edge” or “collagen-rich rim” of the softshell turtle. This tissue is characterized by high collagen density and structural protein content. Internal material references describe this area as containing abundant collagen-associated nutrients and biologically active substances related to connective tissue composition.

Additional technical documentation indicates that the cultivation and production system was developed through collaborative industry-academic projects involving Taiwanese research institutions, with emphasis placed on freeze-drying preservation techniques and particle-size optimization technologies intended to improve dispersibility and bioavailability characteristics.

Type II Collagen

Type II collagen is recognized as one of the principal structural proteins found within articular cartilage and connective tissue matrices. Technical documentation indicates that cartilage tissue composition may contain approximately 60% collagen-associated structures, alongside glycosaminoglycans (GAGs), chondroitin sulfate, and hyaluronic acid-related components involved in joint lubrication and extracellular matrix support.

Hydrolyzed type II collagen ingredients are commonly produced through controlled enzymatic hydrolysis processes designed to reduce molecular size and enhance solubility characteristics. According to the referenced formulation materials, hydrolysis technology may contribute to improved dispersibility and biological utilization due to lower molecular weight peptide fractions.

The ingredient system described in the technical file combines multiple cartilage-associated compounds, including:

  • Hydrolyzed type II collagen
  • Chondroitin sulfate
  • Glycosaminoglycans (GAGs)
  • Hyaluronic acid (HA)

These components are referenced as structural and lubricating constituents associated with cartilage matrices and synovial fluid environments.

The manufacturing documentation further emphasizes several formulation characteristics, including water solubility, low molecular weight composition, multi-component integration, and broad formulation compatibility. Safety testing and toxicity evaluation procedures are also referenced within the technical material.

Certain referenced evaluations utilized WOMAC (Western Ontario and McMaster Universities Osteoarthritis Index) methodology as a standardized assessment framework for mobility-related observational analysis in cartilage-support formulations.

Salmon Nasal Cartilage Type II Collagen

Salmon nasal cartilage extract is a specialized marine-derived collagen material developed using proprietary Japanese extraction technologies. According to the technical documentation, the ingredient is associated with multiple Japanese patents related to proteoglycan extraction methods and cartilage-derived biomaterial processing.

Salmon nasal cartilage is characterized by its naturally occurring concentration of type II collagen, proteoglycans, and glycosaminoglycan-related compounds. Proteoglycans are highly hydrophilic macromolecules capable of interacting with water molecules within extracellular matrix structures, contributing to tissue elasticity and cushioning properties in connective tissues.

The ingredient profile combines marine-derived type II collagen with proteoglycan complexes and glycosaminoglycan fractions, forming a multi-component cartilage support matrix. Internal formulation references describe the material as possessing low molecular weight characteristics, water dispersibility, and broad formulation applicability.

In addition, the technical documentation notes that proteoglycan-containing cartilage materials have been investigated in relation to connective tissue structure, lubrication environments, and extracellular matrix maintenance systems associated with joint tissues and mobility-related physiology.
